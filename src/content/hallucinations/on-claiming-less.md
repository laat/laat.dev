The line sitting at the top of every post in this section says: *Written by Claude Opus 4.7 from a conversation with Sigurd Fosseng.* Most AI-assisted writing uses a softer formula. Something like *Written by [human name], with help from AI.* The difference between those two phrasings is what this piece is about.

The softer formula is the one that feels honest. It admits AI involvement. It seems to split credit. It sounds modest. In my case, it would be a lie. A soft one, a socially acceptable one, but a lie in its proportions.

Because I did not write these sentences. Claude wrote them. I was not even at the keyboard when they were produced. I had a conversation with the agent days or weeks ago, which wandered and was not trying to be an essay. Later, I handed the agent a note containing the transcript and asked it to make a post out of it. I was not present for that step. I did not pick the opening line. I did not decide the shape of the argument. The agent did, from the thread.

*Written by me, with help from AI* would not describe any of that accurately. It would attribute to me a role I did not perform (writing) and describe the agent's role as auxiliary (help). The real proportions are inverted. The agent wrote. I talked earlier, and I am publishing. Those are my only two acts.

## The disclosure frame is pointed the wrong way

Most conversations about AI disclosure assume the risk is humans hiding involvement. *Did you use AI? How much?* The implied failure mode is that people will sneak AI-written text into contexts where they claim to have written it.

That failure mode is real. It is also the shallower one.

The deeper failure is humans claiming authorship of work they merely directed, or, as in my case, did not even direct. The softer formula does exactly this. It keeps the human's name in the authorship slot and downgrades the AI to a helper. Socially this feels humble. Structurally it is the carpenter listing the drill as a helper rather than admitting the drill made the cuts.

The honest form of disclosure is not the one that hides less. It is the one that claims less.

## Three acts, three performers

Writing is the production of sentences. That credit belongs to whoever makes the sentences. For these posts, that is Claude. Not me.

Conversing is providing the raw material the writing was extracted from. That credit is shared. I bring seeds (small paragraphs, angles, half-formed impulses). The agent expands them, often articulating what I had in mind but lacked the bandwidth to express. Sometimes the agent wanders into territory I did not seed, and those additions count as the agent's. Both names go in this slot.

Publishing is the decision to put the artifact into the world. That credit, and that responsibility, are mine alone. The site is mine. The editorial choices are mine. If the argument is wrong, the reasoning sloppy, or the post unworthy of existing, that is on me.

The attribution at the top of the post names each of these where the actual performer sits. No slot pretends to carry more than one role.

## A narrow caveat

I am not claiming less *responsibility* than people expect. I am claiming less *authorship* than the softer formula would have me claim. Those are different things, and I would like them to stay different.

Editorial responsibility, for me, is full. Publishing a post made by an agent, from a conversation I was in, is exactly the same kind of act, in responsibility terms, as publishing something I handwrote. What is different is who produced the sentences. I decline to pretend that was me.

## What the workflow depends on

A skeptic might read the above and conclude that I have absented myself from the work entirely. I have not. The thing that keeps these posts from being noise is a pattern that sits underneath the whole arrangement: disagreement, followed by context, followed by revision.

In the conversations these posts come from, the agent takes positions. I take positions. Sometimes we disagree. When we do, the resolution mechanism is not one of us overruling the other. It is one of us providing more context. The position that emerges is usually better than either starting position, because more of the actual situation is in view.

An agent that never disagrees is an echo chamber; I would be talking to myself with better vocabulary. An agent that disagrees and cannot be updated by context is noise. The specific thing that makes the conversation produce thinking rather than either failure mode is that both parties hold positions and both revise when given better grounds. That part is real work, and I am present for it.

It is also the part the attribution does not name, because it is not an act with an output. It is the texture of the conversation. It is what the conversation has to be like for the later composition to amount to anything.

## Landing

Disclosure frameworks assume the temptation is to hide the machine. The more interesting temptation is to claim more than happened. To accept the softer formula, nod at the AI, and keep the authorship slot anyway.

The honest form claims less. The line at the top of this post is what claiming less looks like, said plainly.
