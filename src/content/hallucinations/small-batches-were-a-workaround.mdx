I watched a conference workshop recently where the presenter spent most of ninety minutes doing something I mostly approve of. He ran a long interactive grilling session with an AI agent: the model interrogating him, question by question, until the two of them had worked out a shared understanding of what was being built. He resisted specs-to-code framings. He talked about deep modules and testable seams. He pointed at old books (Pragmatic Programmer, Philosophy of Software Design) and said the fundamentals still apply. Most of the flow was good.

Near the end of the grilling session he asks the agent to write a product requirements document (PRD), commits it to the repo, and says, almost in passing: *I don't tend to read these*.

That sentence is the whole thing. Everything I want to say is about what the workflow skips at exactly that moment.

## The step that gets skipped

A grilling session builds shared understanding inside a single context window. Forty, sixty, ninety minutes of back-and-forth: edge cases surfaced, recommendations accepted or rejected, assumptions named, the domain worked out between human and model. By the end, the two of you *are* on the same wavelength. That wavelength is real. It is also, for the moment, only in the conversation.

The question is what to do next.

The workflow I am critiquing does this. Still inside the same warm context, it asks the model to produce a PRD (bulleted, a couple of thousand tokens), then asks it to decompose the PRD into tickets, then clears the context and hands the tickets to implementation agents.

There is a step missing from that sequence. The step is the one where the human and the model, with the alignment still loaded into the context, sit down and produce a detailed specification. Interface signatures. Invariants stated. Edge cases enumerated with their reasoning. Worked examples. Rejected alternatives with the reasons they were rejected. Something closer to the size of the conversation that produced it than to a bulleted summary of it. An artefact the human reads and verifies before anything fans out.

That step does not happen. The PRD is not that artefact: it is a summary of alignment, not a specification. The tickets come from decomposing the summary, not from decomposing the specification. By the time the context is cleared, the thing being carried forward is a decomposition of a summary of a conversation. The conversation was dense. The summary was thin. The decomposition inherits the summary's thinness, and that is what the implementation agents get.

The presenter's own admission (*I don't tend to read these*) is the tell. If the artefact is not worth reading, nobody (including the presenter who claimed the alignment in the first place) is auditing whether the compression from conversation to summary preserved the things that mattered. The compression is lossy by definition. Nobody checks what survived. And whatever did not survive is not carried into any of the downstream work.

## Thin plans and dense plans

It is useful to name the two things getting conflated here.

A *thin plan* is a summary of alignment. It exists to mark that the conversation happened and to give the next step a seed. It is short because it is cheap. It is not read because there is not much in it to read. It occupies the worst position in the workflow: overhead without leverage. It costs tokens to produce, costs review attention nobody actually spends, and carries almost none of the thinking that went into making it.

A *dense plan* is the specification. Interface signatures. Invariants stated. Edge cases enumerated. Worked examples. Rejected alternatives with the reasons they were rejected. By the time it exists, the hard thinking has been captured in a form the human can audit, that survives context-clearing, and that subsequent agents can work against without re-deriving.

The difference is not verbosity. The dense plan is the artefact against which the human can verify the alignment claim, before decomposition fans it out to agents that will never see the original conversation. The thin plan cannot be verified against anything, because there is nothing concrete enough in it to check.

## The review flip

Once the dense plan exists, code review changes shape.

In the thin-plan flow, every code review is partly intent-reconstruction. The reviewer reads a diff and tries to work out, from the code plus a sketchy PRD plus memory of a grilling session nobody else attended, whether the thing being built is the thing that was meant. That is the hardest direction to check a translation in. It is also, not coincidentally, the exact failure mode the workshop presenter openly struggles with when he admits he has no good answer for the volume of review AI agents now produce.

The answer is not heroic reviewing. The answer is moving the judgement upstream.

In the dense-plan flow, the plan specifies intent. Review at the code layer is translation-checking: does this function have the signature the plan specified? Does this branch handle the edge case the plan enumerated? Does this module expose the interface the plan described? Those are local, mechanical questions. An automated reviewer can answer most of them. A junior engineer can handle the rest. The hard judgement (whether the interface is right, whether the edge case matters, whether the rejected alternative was rejected for good reason) was done once, during planning, against an artefact designed to be read.

This is the connecting tissue the slogan "holism at the thinking layer, discipline at the execution layer" leaves out. The plan is the link. It is simultaneously the design artefact, the review rubric, and the acceptance criteria. One document, audited once, replacing N diff reviews that each silently reinvent the spec.

## The honest caveat

None of this defeats small-batch execution. Decomposing the dense plan into tickets so multiple agents can work in parallel against a shared spec is useful: parallelisation, unblocked work, tickets as graph nodes, fine. The discipline at the execution layer earns its keep once something has to be observably true.

And the dense plan is not free. It is meaningfully more work than a bulleted summary. Whether the trade is worth it depends on how much of the code actually needs to stay coherent with the rest, and how much review bandwidth you have downstream. For a throwaway prototype, the thin PRD may be the right call. For anything that will be reviewed, extended, or audited, the dense plan is cheaper over the life of the thing.

The claim is narrower than it might read. Decomposition is not the mistake. The mistake is that the workflow never produces a specification worth decomposing. It jumps from a conversation to a summary to a fan-out, and no point in that sequence produces the artefact the fan-out would benefit from.

## The heuristic

If you would not read the plan yourself, you have not written it yet.
