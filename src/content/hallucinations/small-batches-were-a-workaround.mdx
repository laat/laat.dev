There is a kind of class in legacy codebases that everyone recognises. It is called something generic like `OrderService` or `UserManager`. It does too much, but nothing quite enough. Its methods take bags of optional parameters because the interface had to accommodate callers it was not designed for. Reading it feels like archaeology: you can see the layers where someone added a new responsibility without deciding what the class actually was.

The code did not come out like that by accident. Someone wrote it. And at some point before they wrote it, they decided, consciously or not, to skip the part where they worked out what the thing actually was. The code is downstream of that decision. The pathology is always downstream.

## The work that does not look like work

The hard work in engineering is the judgement work. Deciding which interfaces are right. Deciding which edge cases matter and which can be deferred. Deciding which alternatives to reject and being able to state why. Working out which invariants the thing will maintain, and what happens when they do not. Holding the shape of the system in your head long enough for the shape to come into focus.

This work has a problem. It produces almost nothing. At the end of a good session of it, you have a head that knows things it did not know before, and possibly a few pages of notes that only make sense to you. You do not have a deliverable. You cannot show it in a standup. You cannot close a ticket with it. You can't even be sure, retrospectively, that it happened, in the way you can be sure a commit happened.

So there is always economic pressure to do something else instead. Something that looks like progress. A design doc. A spike. A pull request. A grooming session. A product requirements document (PRD). A ticket board. These are not bad artefacts. Some are quite good. But they are all, in their way, easier than sitting in the unresolved middle of the problem until the answer comes. They all produce something you can point at, and a thing you can point at is reassuring, and reassurance is the thing being optimised for whether anyone admits it or not.

The skip is the decision, usually implicit, to take one of these and call it finished. To treat the artefact as the work.

## It showed before AI

Every code pathology any of us knows by name is evidence of a skip somewhere upstream.

Accidental complexity: someone modelled the solution before they understood the problem. Leaky abstraction: someone named a boundary without working out what it was actually separating. God class: someone kept accreting responsibility because deciding where it belonged would have required thinking. Over-engineering: someone designed for a future they had not actually reasoned about. Under-engineering: someone designed for the demo and stopped. Interfaces that work for one caller and resist the second. Types that almost encode an invariant. Comments that disagree with the code. Tests that assert the implementation rather than the behaviour.

The shared root is the same. At some point the author was supposed to sit with the problem and did not. The pathology is the shape of the hole the thinking would have filled.

We have acquired a whole literature around the pathologies: catalogues, refactorings, smells, principles, named anti-patterns. Most of it is downstream advice. It is about what to do once the skip has already happened and its effects are in the repo. Some of it is quite good. None of it is a substitute for not having skipped in the first place.

## What AI changed is the economics

The skip was always cheap. AI made it radically cheaper.

Everything that looks like progress now generates in seconds. Grilling sessions, PRDs, tickets, scaffolded code, tests that pass, commits, whole subsystems. The artefact rate went up by an order of magnitude. The rate of actual judgement did not, because judgement is rate-limited by the human who has to do it, and no model runs that loop on your behalf. A model can hold context, press you on edge cases, list alternatives, write out the specification once you have actually decided. It cannot decide.

This shifts the ratio between work-that-looks-like-progress and work-that-is-progress in an unhappy direction. The looks-like side got enormously cheaper. The is-progress side costs exactly what it always cost. So skipping is now easier to choose than it has ever been, because the skip-substitutes are more convincing than ever. A flawless-looking PRD generated in thirty seconds feels more like finished thinking than a messy hour of argument with yourself. It is not.

And the failure mode amplifies. The old skip produced one confused class and some bad tests. The new skip produces a fifty-thousand-line implementation of a confused specification, across a branch backlog nobody has the bandwidth to review. The workshop presenter who admits he has no answer to the volume of AI-generated code coming back at him is not facing a new problem. He is facing the old skip, multiplied by the new generation rate, and noticing (correctly) that the old tools do not scale to meet it.

## The honest caveat

None of this is an argument for heroic up-front design, or for treating every feature as a months-long thinking retreat. A lot of programming is routine enough that the judgement has already been done, by convention, by the framework, by the codebase. Skipping the non-existent is not skipping.

And the hard work I am pointing at is not just "more planning." Planning artefacts can themselves be skip-substitutes, and frequently are. The test is not whether a document exists. The test is whether someone sat with the problem long enough to know what the thing should be, and can answer questions about it that were not in the document. If they cannot, the document was the skip, not the work.

## What the quality is telling you

This is the uncomfortable part. Quality has always been the tell for whether the judgement work happened. It shows up in the shape of interfaces, in the handling of edge cases, in what the error messages say, in whether the names hold up under pressure, in whether the invariants are real or aspirational. You can read a codebase and, within a few hundred lines, feel whether someone thought.

AI does not change the reading. It changes the scale. When the generation rate goes up, the quality signal gets easier to see, not harder. The skipped work is still skipped. It is just skipped louder.

Quality is downstream of thinking. It always was. AI only changed how loud the skip is.
