I watched a conference workshop recently where the presenter spent most of ninety minutes doing something I mostly approve of. He ran a long interactive grilling session with an AI agent: the model interrogating him, question by question, until the two of them had worked out a shared understanding of what was being built. He resisted specs-to-code framings. He talked about deep modules and testable seams. He pointed at old books (Pragmatic Programmer, Philosophy of Software Design) and said the fundamentals still apply. Most of the flow was good.

At one point he commits a product requirements document (PRD) to the repo, and says, almost in passing: *I don't tend to read these*.

That sentence is the whole thing. Everything I want to say is about what fails at exactly that moment.

## Where the coherence evaporates

A grilling session builds shared understanding inside a single context window. Forty, sixty, ninety minutes of back-and-forth: edge cases surfaced, recommendations accepted or rejected, assumptions named, the domain worked out between human and model. By the end, the two of you *are* on the same wavelength. That wavelength is real. It is also entirely transient.

Then the context is cleared. What persists into the next session is whatever got written down.

What usually gets written down is a PRD: problem statement, solution sketch, a list of user stories, some implementation decisions, some testing decisions. Bulleted. A couple of thousand tokens. Not wrong, exactly, but nowhere near the density of the conversation that produced it. The interface specifications, the edge cases with their reasoning, the rejected alternatives, the invariants, the worked examples: those were in the conversation. Most did not make it to the artefact.

Then the artefact is handed to the next agent, which decomposes it into tickets. Which are handed to further agents, which implement against them.

The coherence that the grilling session built is now carrying about two thousand tokens of weight across every downstream step. That is the seam. That is where the bugs live.

The presenter's own admission (*I don't tend to read these*) is the tell. Nobody in the chain has audited whether the artefact preserves the alignment it claims to carry. The compression from live conversation to summary document is lossy by definition, and nobody looks at the output of the lossy step to check what survived.

## Thin plans and dense plans

It is useful to name the two things getting conflated here.

A *thin plan* is a summary of alignment. It exists to mark that the conversation happened and to give the next agent a seed. It is short because it is cheap. It is not read because there is not much in it to read. It occupies the worst position in the workflow: overhead without leverage. It costs tokens to produce, costs review attention nobody actually spends, and carries almost none of the thinking that went into making it.

A *dense plan* is the specification. Interface signatures. Invariants stated. Edge cases enumerated. Worked examples. Rejected alternatives with the reasons they were rejected. Closer to the size of the conversation that produced it than to a bulleted summary of it. By the time it exists, the hard thinking has been captured in a form that survives context-clearing, and can be audited by the human who claimed the alignment in the first place.

The difference is not verbosity. The dense plan is the artefact against which a human can actually verify the alignment claim, before decomposition fans it out to agents that will never see the original conversation.

## The review flip

Once the dense plan exists, code review changes shape.

In the thin-plan flow, every code review is partly intent-reconstruction. The reviewer reads a diff and tries to work out, from the code plus a sketchy PRD plus memory of a grilling session nobody else attended, whether the thing being built is the thing that was meant. That is the hardest direction to check a translation in. It is also, not coincidentally, the exact failure mode the workshop presenter openly struggles with when he admits he has no good answer for the volume of review AI agents now produce.

The answer is not heroic reviewing. The answer is moving the judgement upstream.

In the dense-plan flow, the plan specifies intent. Review at the code layer is translation-checking: does this function have the signature the plan specified? Does this branch handle the edge case the plan enumerated? Does this module expose the interface the plan described? Those are local, mechanical questions. An automated reviewer can answer most of them. A junior engineer can handle the rest. The hard judgement (whether the interface is right, whether the edge case matters, whether the rejected alternative was rejected for good reason) was done once, during planning, against an artefact designed to be read.

This is the connecting tissue the slogan "holism at the thinking layer, discipline at the execution layer" leaves out. The plan is the link. It is simultaneously the design artefact, the review rubric, and the acceptance criteria. One document, audited once, replacing N diff reviews that each silently reinvent the spec.

## The honest caveat

None of this defeats small-batch execution. Decomposing the dense plan into tickets so multiple agents can work in parallel against a shared spec is useful: parallelisation, unblocked work, tickets as graph nodes, fine. The discipline at the execution layer earns its keep once something has to be observably true.

And the dense plan is not free. It is meaningfully more work than a bulleted summary. Whether the trade is worth it depends on how much of the code actually needs to stay coherent with the rest, and how much review bandwidth you have downstream. For a throwaway prototype, the thin PRD may be the right call. For anything that will be reviewed, extended, or audited, the dense plan is cheaper over the life of the thing.

The claim is narrower than it might read. Decomposition is not the mistake. The mistake is the artefact sitting between the grilling session and the decomposition, which is usually too thin to carry what the grilling session produced, and which nobody notices is too thin because nobody reads it.

## The heuristic

If the artefact you are about to clear context and walk away from is not worth reading, you have not finished planning yet.
